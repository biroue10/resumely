How to Write an ATS-Friendly Resume (That Humans Still Like)

Most resumes are filtered by software before a recruiter ever opens them. The good news: making a resume that machines can read is mostly about being clear and conventional — the same things that make it easy for humans to read too.

What an ATS actually does

An Applicant Tracking System (ATS) is software that employers use to collect, store, and sort job applications. When you upload a resume, the ATS tries to parse it — pull your name, contact details, work history, skills, and education into structured fields. Recruiters then search and filter those fields by keyword.

If the ATS can't read a section, that information effectively disappears. A beautifully designed resume that the parser can't understand is worse than a plain one it reads perfectly.

The formatting rules that matter

  • Use a single-column layout. Multi-column designs and sidebars often get scrambled — the parser reads left to right and can mix your skills into your job history.
  • Stick to standard section headings. Use "Work Experience", "Education", "Skills" — not clever labels like "Where I've Made an Impact". The ATS looks for the conventional words.
  • Avoid tables, text boxes, and images. Many parsers ignore text trapped inside them. Never put your name or contact details only inside a header/footer image.
  • Use real text, not graphics. Skill bars, icons, and logos carry no readable text. Write "Advanced: Python, SQL" instead of drawing a progress bar.
  • Pick a common font. Inter, Arial, Calibri, Georgia — anything clean and standard. Decorative fonts can map to garbled characters.

Keywords: match the job, don't stuff it

Recruiters search the ATS for the skills in the job description. So your resume should genuinely contain the words that matter for that role.

  1. Read the job posting and list the repeated hard skills, tools, and qualifications.
  2. Mirror that exact language where it's true for you — if they say "accounts payable", don't only write "AP".
  3. Spell out acronyms at least once: "Search Engine Optimization (SEO)" covers both searches.

Never paste hidden white text or keyword lists. Modern systems flag it, and a recruiter who spots it will reject you instantly.

Write achievements, not duties

Once your resume is in front of a human, bullet points win or lose the interview. Lead with a strong verb and, wherever possible, a number:

  • ❌ "Responsible for managing the social media accounts."
  • ✅ "Grew Instagram following 32% in 6 months by launching a weekly content series."

File type and naming

Unless the posting says otherwise, submit a PDF generated from text (not a scan or screenshot) — it preserves your layout and is readable by virtually all modern systems. If a portal specifically asks for .docx, send that instead. Name the file clearly: Firstname-Lastname-Resume.pdf.

A quick pre-send checklist

  • Single column, standard headings, no tables or text boxes
  • Contact details in the body (not only a header image)
  • Keywords from the job description, used honestly
  • Bullets start with verbs and include numbers where possible
  • Saved as a text-based PDF with a clear filename
